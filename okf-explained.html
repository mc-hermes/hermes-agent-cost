The 90-Second Explainer

Google just told the world how AI should store knowledge.

Here's what it means for your business — in plain English.

90 seconds · 6 questions

What happened?
June 2026: Google published OKF (Open Knowledge Format). It says: AI agents should store business knowledge as plain text files. That's it. No special software. No vendor lock-in. Readable by any text editor.
Why does this matter to me?
Your business knowledge — client history, pricing decisions, project notes — is scattered across WhatsApp, email, and people's heads. When you try to use AI to answer a business question, it can't find anything. OKF gives that knowledge a standard home that both humans and AI can read.
Do I need to buy a Google product?
No. OKF is a free, open standard — like PDF or CSV. Google wrote the spec. They don't host it, sell it, or touch your data. Your knowledge lives in your own files, on your own infrastructure.
What's the catch?
OKF defines the output format. It doesn't solve the hard part: getting your knowledge in. Someone still needs to build the pipeline that takes your emails, meeting notes, and WhatsApp threads and turns them into structured knowledge. The format is free. The pipeline is the work.
Is anyone actually doing this?
Yes — at scale. Garry Tan (CEO of Y Combinator) runs his entire operation on gbrain, an open-source knowledge engine built on exactly this pattern: 146,000+ pages, 24,000+ people tracked, 66 autonomous jobs running nightly. Google just blessed the format he was already using.
