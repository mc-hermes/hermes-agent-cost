The 90-Second Explainer

Google just told the world how AI should store knowledge.

Here's what it means for your business — no jargon.

90 seconds · 6 questions

What happened?
Google published a standard called OKF (Open Knowledge Format). It says: "AI agents should store knowledge as plain text files. Markdown. That's it." No special software. No vendor lock-in. Anyone can read it with any text editor.
Why should I care?
Because your business knowledge is currently scattered across email, Slack, meeting notes, and people's heads. When you want to use AI to answer a business question, it can't find anything. OKF gives all that knowledge a single, clean home that both you and AI can read.
Is this a Google product I have to buy?
No. It's a format, like PDF or CSV. It's free, open, and works anywhere. Google just wrote the spec — they don't host it, sell it, or own your data.
What's the catch?
OKF defines the output format. It doesn't solve the hard part: getting your knowledge in. Someone still has to feed the brain. Emails, transcripts, invoices — they don't magically become markdown files. That ingestion step is the real work.
So what do I actually need?
Three things: (1) a knowledge format (OKF — solved by Google, free), (2) a knowledge engine (gbrain — open-source, production-proven), and (3) a pipeline that feeds your emails and documents into it automatically (this is what we build).
How is this different from Notion or a wiki?
Notion and wikis are manual. Someone has to write, tag, and update everything. Humans stop doing that after about 3 weeks. OKF + gbrain is autonomous — the AI reads your forwarded emails, extracts what matters, links it to your existing knowledge, and enriches it overnight. It's a wiki that writes itself.

The bottom line: Google standardised the format. The open-source community built the engine. We built the pipeline that feeds it — so you don't have to. Forward an email. Ask it anything. Your business knowledge compounds every day you own it.
